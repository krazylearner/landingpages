Best Practices for Surfing Safely

It's a common scenario: You've opened an email from a credit card provider or social media network telling you that your password was breached or you need to update your account information. You're about to click on the email link when something stops you. Maybe it's the sender's address, which doesn't match the organization that's purporting to be contacting you. Or the business logo looks different than you remember. You close the email and realize you were nearly scammed. Never fall for the scam when you know these best practices for browsing the web safely.

Safe Web Browsing

When you go online to surf the web, remain vigilant and be suspicious of advertisements that pop up. Before you provide your information to enter a contest, download a piece of software, or do anything else, make sure that the offer is legitimate.

Likewise, suspicious errors - like browser alerts that your antivirus software may be out of date - can be scams. Do not respond to in-browser error alerts like these.

Avoid downloading files from the internet unless you know they are safe. .Zip files or mp.3s from unauthorized providers could contain spyware that tracks your behavior.

Any website that asks for your personal information should have a small lock in the corner of the browser bar and begin with "https" instead of just "http." The "s" stands for secure and indicates that your personal information is protected during transmission and that third parties cannot access it.

Older browsers may be vulnerable to malware. If your computer alerts you that the browser needs to be updated, or there is a patch available, take the time to do it to protect yourself.

Safe browsing habits offer a first line of defense against malware, but cannot keep you safe alone. Use an antivirus software for backup protection. This way, if you download a file that contains a virus, you'll be alerted to the suspicious file before it can cause you harm.

Safe Email Practices

Your email provider should perform baseline spam filtering that keeps your inbox free of offers for pharmaceuticals or free credit cards. Even the best spam filter isn't perfect, and sometimes spam can get in. When you see an email you know to be spam, delete it without opening it. If you see a suspicious email - for example, an email from a sender you don't recognize - do not open it.

If you open an email from a known party directing you to visit a website, type the website URL into the browser bar instead of simply clicking on the link. While this seems like extra work for you, it protects you from accidentally visiting an inauthentic site and falling prey to a phishing scam.

These tips will help you stay safe from harm. To protect yourself, you will also need to stay up to date with news about phishing scams, viruses, and malware and continue to follow best practices as new threats evolve.
